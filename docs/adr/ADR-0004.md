# ADR-0004: Segment Identity Within the Aggregate

**Status:** Accepted  
**Date:** 2026-07-21

---

## Context

`SegmentDNA` represents a structural region of a song.

Within EchoInsight's domain model, segments are owned by `StructureDNA`, which is itself owned by the `SongDNA` aggregate (see ADR-0001).

From a strict Domain-Driven Design perspective, child entities do not normally reference their aggregate root. Ownership flows from parent to child, and navigation typically occurs through the aggregate.

However, EchoInsight performs many operations whose natural output is a collection of individual segments rather than complete songs.

Examples include:

- segment similarity search
- recommendation
- comparison results
- dataset generation
- analytics pipelines

Once a segment leaves the aggregate boundary, it still needs to retain enough identity to remain meaningful.

The architectural question therefore became:

> Should a `SegmentDNA` carry the identity of the song that owns it?

---

## Decision Drivers

The decision prioritized:

- Practical usability
- Stable identity
- Efficient lookup
- Simplicity of downstream APIs
- Preservation of aggregate integrity

---

## Decision

`SegmentDNA` contains a lightweight `song_id` identifying the aggregate that owns it.

```text
SongDNA
└── StructureDNA
    └── SegmentDNA
        └── song_id
```

This does **not** create a bidirectional object relationship.

`SegmentDNA` does not hold a reference to its parent object.

Instead, it carries only the immutable identifier of the aggregate from which it originated.

The identifier allows a segment to remain meaningful when transported independently through comparison engines, recommendation pipelines, or search results.

Ownership remains unchanged.

`SongDNA` is still the aggregate root responsible for validating consistency and lifecycle.

---

## Alternatives Considered

### Strict Aggregate Ownership

Segments contain no reference to their parent.

Consumers are responsible for maintaining the relationship between segments and songs.

**Rejected because**

Once segments leave the aggregate, every downstream consumer must reconstruct that relationship independently.

This duplicates logic across services and complicates otherwise simple APIs.

Identity becomes external rather than intrinsic.

---

### Wrapper Objects Only

Service-layer types such as `SegmentMatch` contain the owning `song_id`, while `SegmentDNA` itself remains unaware of its origin.

**Rejected because**

Wrapper types solve only a subset of use cases.

`SegmentDNA` frequently exists outside comparison services, including testing, serialization, experimentation, and future processing pipelines.

Embedding stable ownership identity directly into the domain object produces a more self-contained and reusable model.

Wrapper objects remain useful, but they complement rather than replace domain identity.

---

## Consequences

### Advantages

- Every segment carries stable ownership identity.
- Downstream services can reason about segments without reconstructing aggregate relationships.
- Search and recommendation APIs become significantly simpler.
- Aggregate validation can verify ownership consistency.
- Consumers can defer loading complete songs until necessary.

### Disadvantages

- The model departs slightly from strict Domain-Driven Design purity.
- The aggregate duplicates a small amount of information (`song_id`) across owned segments.
- Ownership consistency must be validated when constructing the aggregate.

### Tradeoffs

EchoInsight intentionally favors practical engineering over absolute adherence to DDD theory.

The duplicated identifier is small, immutable, and inexpensive to validate.

The resulting APIs are significantly simpler while preserving the aggregate boundary established in ADR-0001.

---

## Future Considerations

If EchoInsight later adopts relational persistence, `song_id` naturally becomes the persistence-layer foreign key while remaining the stable domain identifier.

This decision therefore scales across both file-based and database-backed storage without requiring changes to the domain model.
